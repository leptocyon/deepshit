Wildlife Are in Deep Shit.

How Factory Farm Pollution Threatens Imperiled Species


start

Farm animals produce an astonishing amount of waste — about 1.4 trillion pounds a year in the United States alone. This waste, largely produced in industrial animal-agriculture operations, also called "factory farms," contributes to climate change and pollutes waterways, natural habitats and ecosystems, putting wildlife in deep sh*t.

Sensitive wildlife species are the overlooked victims of America's factory farms. See our species page to learn more about wildlife harmed by livestock manure.

Monarch butterflies, Delmarva fox squirrels, green sea turtles and yellow-billed cuckoos are all threatened by industrial animal waste in their habitats.


Wildlife across the United States is threatened by industrial meat and dairy production: 29 states identify factory farms as the leading contributor to water pollution.


Manure lagoons of swine farms in a section of the Cape Fear watershed.

Animal waste usually goes virtually untreated in open, pit-like holes or mountainous piles. Raw animal waste is then spread or sprayed onto nearby fields, transferring toxic materials — such as pesticides, pharmaceuticals, pathogens, heavy metals and nutrient pollution — into the soil. Sometimes the waste is even dumped into waterways intentionally.


From top left in clock-wise order: 1) Aerial view of a manure lagoon by cattle feedlot, 2) liquid waste being sprayed on agricultural field, 3) manure pit, 4) tanker spraying liquid waste on agricultural field.

The pharmaceuticals and pesticides fed to factory farm animals can enter the groundwater through their waste, and contribute to the spread of antibiotic-resistant bacteria and incidents of endocrine and reproductive disruption in people and wildlife alike.

In 2016 Hurricane Matthew flooded many of North Carolina's industrial hog farms and spilled waste from massive manure lagoons into nearby waterways.

This pollution is driven by the super-sized American appetite for meat and dairy. To meet the demand, livestock production has evolved from small family farms to massive industrial animal factories with enormous concentrations of noxious environmental pollutants.

Manure from industrial animal operations is produced in vastly larger quantities than human excrement in regions where livestock farming is common.

Wildlife in Deep Shit, a report by the Center for Biological Diversity, highlights the top 10 places in the United States where wildlife are in deep sh*t from the environmental destruction caused by factory farms. This map, the first of its kind, shows manure data and the threatened wildlife that may be affected by it on a county level.

Livestock manure also contributes to climate change: According to the FAO, it's responsible for more than a quarter of animal agriculture's global greenhouse gases.

There are very few meaningful legal protections for wildlife or humans from the damages caused by factory farms and their waste. Most factory farms aren't even required by the EPA to operate under a Clean Water Act permit.

Help get wildlife and our waterways out of deep sh*t:

Tell the EPA to regulate factory farms and protect our water.
Eat more plant-based meals and less meat and dairy to reduce the demand for factory farm production.
